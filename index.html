Faustian Tech Debt

Published: Wed 16 June 2021
By Maxwell G Brown

In rants.

Faustian Tech Debt

Many of the failed software projects I've worked on cemented it's downard trajectory with the same well-intentioned trade off: "If we just stop doing X then we'll be able to get this through!"

Let me name a few from experience: - Merge code without review - Skip writing tests - Disable checks in the CI so it'll pass - Push right to develop - ...I'm sure there are more

All of the above share the same intention: temporarily remove some of the overhead so that developers can deliver features in a more timely fashion.

The problem is not with intention (we all love Code Review & Testing (right?!)). The problem is with reality.

This is faustian tech debt.

"Don't you think that's an exaggeration? We can always come back and re-address it!"

I meant what I said; the "debt" we now owe our soul to the devil.

In my experience there is no returning from this point. Once the proper practices have been eschewed to hit a deadline for a release, you have reached the point of no return.

I've even seen disciplined teams fail to recover from this: "If we write a ticket to address what we've just undone, then we can plan on readdressing it!"

No, in reality that ticket will be forever be deprioritized and you can stare at it loggingly after you scroll to the bottom of the backlog: "Oh man, I wish we had never sold our souls to the devil".

The Agile Manifesto Denounces the Devil!

Nothing in the Agile Manifesto mentions timelines and project management, but it does advocate for continuous delivery. And not just in one of the principles, but in several!

Our highest priority is to satisfy the customer through early and continuous delivery of valuable software.

Deliver working software frequently, from a couple of weeks to a couple of months, with a preference to the shorter timescale.

Agile processes promote sustainable development. The sponsors, developers, and users should be able to maintain a constant pace indefinitely.

Continuous attention to technical excellence and good design enhances agility.

I'll keep my point brief and simple: if at any point you consider shortcutting your development process then you are not properly practicing agile development.

Short aside on project management fallacies

I've worked on plenty of projects that completely eschew this though: "We want it on this date, we want exactly these features, and we won't touch resourcing."

To anybody with a working knowledge of project management, what they just heard was "I want it cheap, fast, and I want it all." which is a complete violation of the Triple Constraint philosophy.

Ineviably, what happens in the end is that something gives: - The scope is less than promised (which leads to another cycle of "get these other features NOW!") - The release date gets pushed back (and you already sold yourself short! Why'd we sacrifice our process again?)

and regardless of what gives, everybody walks away embarrassed.

I posit that nobody properly practicing agile would find thmselves in this scenario.

In Conclusion...

Don't destroy your process just to meet a deadline. Instad destroy the system which set a deadline that you cannot met without destroying your processes.
